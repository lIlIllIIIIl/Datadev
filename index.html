CLIMATE IMPACT BY MEDIA

The Project

We analyzed how French digital media outlets wrote and displayed information about the origin and processing of electricity. Many maps and photographs were used to illustrate the journalist's point, while detailed graphics were less common.

For our benchmark, we looked at various sources, from word clouds commonly used to map the links between Twitch streamers, to Dr. Tufte's pie charts. If we take a moment to examine these graphs, we may notice a shimmering or vibrating effect known as the Moiré Effect. Replacing patterns with shading or colors simplifies the graphs and makes them much cleaner.

When embarking on our project, we faced the challenge of sourcing relevant data for our analysis. As we were interested in analyzing the semantic text of articles discussing the origin of electricity in France, we quickly realized that there were no existing automated methods that could accurately perform this task.

Although we initially considered automating data scraping, we found that the complexity of the task made it difficult to implement in practice.

Let's start

DATA

As a result, we decided to manually process the data by analyzing more than thirty articles. To ensure consistency and avoid biases, we pre-defined around forty key words to analyze. This approach allowed us to gain a deeper understanding of the opinions and text structures presented in each article.

Overall, our manual data processing approach enabled us to identify trends and insights that would have been challenging to obtain through automated methods alone.

Negative/positive reaction to the origin of electricity by the French media and suppliers

DATA

Through our project, we discovered that media outlets and electricity providers have distinct tonalities when it comes to discussing the origin of electricity in France. The media tends to take a critical stance and highlight the ecological impact of electricity production, while electricity providers focus more on promoting their efforts towards sustainability and environmental responsibility.

By analyzing the grammar and vocabulary used in these articles, we were able to identify and compare the various approaches taken by different media outlets and electricity providers. This provided us with valuable insights into how these entities communicate with their respective audiences, and how they frame the issue of electricity production in France.

The results of our analysis underscore the importance of understanding how media outlets and corporations use language to shape public discourse and perception. Such insights can be useful for policymakers, advocacy groups, and other stakeholders who are interested in promoting more informed and responsible public discourse around critical environmental issues such as electricity production.
